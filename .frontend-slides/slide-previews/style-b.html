Topic 1

Introducing Time Series Analysis

Reviewing the statistical foundations, and motivating why time-ordered data demands its own techniques.

Basic concepts

What is a random variable?
  • A random variable is a function $Y : \Omega \mapsto \mathbb{R}$ mapping the outcomes of an experiment to a real number.
  • Denoted by capital italic Roman letters such as $Y = Y(\omega)$, where $\omega \in \Omega$.
  • For a coin toss with outcomes $H,T$: $$Y = 1 \text{ if } H, \quad Y = 0 \text{ if } T$$ defines a random variable.
A real-valued function of the sample space.
What is a stochastic process?
  • A collection of random variables $$\{Y_i : i \in \mathbb{Z}\} \quad\text{or}\quad \{Y_i\}_{i \in \mathbb{Z}}.$$
  • Data $\{y_1,\ldots,y_N\}$ is modelled as a finite realisation of this process.
A collection of random variables.

Students sometimes ask why I present an $iid$ process as a time series after emphasising dependence. An $iid$ process indexed by time is simply a special case — one where dependence happens to be zero.

A stark message

Why is the sample mean ubiquitous?

Theorem — Chebyshev's LLN. If $Y_t \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} F_Y(\mu_Y,\sigma_Y^2)$ with $0<\sigma_Y^2<\infty$, then $$\operatorname*{plim}_{T\to\infty}\bar{Y}_T = \mu_Y.$$

That is, $\bar{Y}_T \overset{p}{\to}\mu_Y$ — the sample mean is consistent for the population mean.

A sample moment converges in probability to its population counterpart.
▸ Intuition

You would never omit cross-terms when squaring: $(a+b+c)^2$ has nine terms. Yet the same slip appears with variances — $\mathsf{Var}(X+Y)\neq\mathsf{Var}(X)+\mathsf{Var}(Y)$ in general.
