Vector autoregressions and structural VARs

Topic 8

Goals

  • To generalise our univariate \(\mathsf{AR}\) framework to the multivariate setting via vector autoregressions (VARs).
  • To state weak stationarity and the stationarity/causality condition for vector processes.
  • To understand the identification problem in structural VARs, and two canonical solutions: short-run (Sims, 1980) and long-run (Blanchard & Quah, 1989) restrictions.
  • To compute impulse response functions and the forecast-error variance decomposition.

Key Equation

\[\mathbf{y}_t = A_1 \mathbf{y}_{t-1} + \mathbf{e}_t, \qquad \text{i.e.} \qquad A(L)\,\mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{e}_t.\]

This topic is adapted from the multivariate slide pack (Part II: Multivariate Series — Selected Topics). Vectors are bold lowercase (\(\mathbf{y}_t, \mathbf{e}_t\)), matrices uppercase (\(A, \Phi, \Sigma, C\)), \(L\) is the lag operator, and \(I_N\) is the \(N \times N\) identity.

1 Vector-valued series

Univariate models — as investigated so far — are useful for the dynamics of individual variables and serve as a great basis for learning the language of time series analysis. But we are often interested not only in a single series but in the relationships among several (e.g. in macroeconomics). Univariate \(\mathsf{AR}\) models necessarily ignore inter-relationships between variables; vector autoregressions (VARs) model the dynamics of \(N\) variables simultaneously, allowing each to depend on its own lagged values and the lagged values of the other \(N-1\) variables.

For example, a two-dimensional \(\mathsf{VAR}(1)\) is \[\begin{aligned} y_{1,t} &= a_{11} y_{1,t-1} + a_{12} y_{2,t-1} + e_{1,t}, \\ y_{2,t} &= a_{21} y_{1,t-1} + a_{22} y_{2,t-1} + e_{2,t}, \end{aligned}\] or more concisely \(A(L)\mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{e}_t\), where \(\mathbf{y}_t = (y_{1,t}, y_{2,t})'\), \(\mathbf{e}_t = (e_{1,t}, e_{2,t})'\) and \[ A(L) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 - a_{11}L & -a_{12}L \\ -a_{21}L & 1 - a_{22}L \end{pmatrix} = I_2 - A_1 L, \quad A_1 = \begin{pmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} \end{pmatrix}. \] So yet another way to write the system is \(\mathbf{y}_t = A_1 \mathbf{y}_{t-1} + \mathbf{e}_t\). It is a good idea to get comfortable with all these notational styles — scalars, vectors, coefficient matrices, and matrix lag polynomials.

What does weak stationarity mean for a vector process?

A process \(\{\mathbf{x}_t\}\) with \(\mathbf{x}_t = (x_{t1}, \ldots, x_{tN})'\) is weakly stationary if

  1. \(\mathsf{E}(\mathbf{x}_t) = \boldsymbol{\mu}\) (constant, with finite entries); and
  2. \(\text{Cov}(\mathbf{x}_t, \mathbf{x}_{t+\tau})\) has finite entries and depends only on \(\tau\).

We collect the (cross-)autocovariances in the autocovariance matrix at lag \(\tau\), \[ \Gamma_\tau = \text{Cov}(\mathbf{x}_t, \mathbf{x}_{t+\tau}), \qquad \text{with } \Gamma_{-\tau} = \Gamma_\tau', \] whose diagonal entries are the individual autocovariances and whose off-diagonal entries are the cross-covariances. The corresponding correlation matrix is \(\rho_\tau = D^{-1/2}\Gamma_\tau D^{-1/2}\), where \(D = \text{diag}(\Gamma_0)\).

A vector process is weakly stationary when its mean and all lag-\(\tau\) (cross-)covariances are time-invariant.

We say \(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t \sim \mathsf{WN}(\mathbf{0}, \Sigma)\) is multivariate white noise if \(\Gamma_\tau = 0\) for \(\tau \neq 0\) (and \(\mathsf{E}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t) = \mathbf{0}\)).

What is a \(\mathsf{VARMA}(p,q)\) process, and when is it stationary?

Definition. \(\{\mathbf{x}_t\}\) is a \(\mathsf{VARMA}(p,q)\) process if \[ \mathbf{x}_t = \Phi_1 \mathbf{x}_{t-1} + \cdots + \Phi_p \mathbf{x}_{t-p} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t + \Theta_1 \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{t-1} + \cdots + \Theta_q \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{t-q}, \] where the \(\Phi_i, \Theta_j\) are \(N \times N\) matrices and \(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t \sim \mathsf{WN}(\mathbf{0}, \Sigma)\). \(\qquad\square\)

The process is weakly stationary and causal if and only if \[ \det\big(I_N - \Phi_1 z - \cdots - \Phi_p z^p\big) \neq 0 \quad \text{for all } |z| \leq 1, \] i.e. all roots of the characteristic polynomial lie outside the unit circle. When stationary, it admits the causal \(\mathsf{VMA}(\infty)\) representation \(\mathbf{x}_t = \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \Psi_j \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{t-j}\).

Same idea as the scalar case — but the stability condition is now a determinant equation.

A numerical example. Consider the \(\mathsf{VAR}(1)\) with \[ \Phi_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0.9 & 0.6 \\ -0.3 & 0.7 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \det(I_2 - \Phi_1 z) = 1 - 1.6 z + 0.81 z^2. \] The roots are \(z \approx 0.99 \pm 0.51 i\), with modulus \(\approx 1.11 > 1\) (outside the unit circle). Therefore \(\{\mathbf{x}_t\}\) is weakly stationary.

Why is the \(\mathsf{VAR}(1)\) our workhorse?

Any \(\mathsf{VAR}(p)\) with \(p > 1\) can be rewritten as a companion \(\mathsf{VAR}(1)\) in higher dimensions. For a 2-D \(\mathsf{VAR}(2)\), \(\mathbf{y}_t = A_1 \mathbf{y}_{t-1} + A_2 \mathbf{y}_{t-2} + \mathbf{e}_t\), stack the lags into \(\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_t = (y_{1,t}, y_{2,t}, y_{1,t-1}, y_{2,t-1})'\): \[ \begin{pmatrix} y_{1,t} \\ y_{2,t} \\ y_{1,t-1} \\ y_{2,t-1} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} A_1 & A_2 \\ I_2 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} y_{1,t-1} \\ y_{2,t-1} \\ y_{1,t-2} \\ y_{2,t-2} \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} e_{1,t} \\ e_{2,t} \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \] i.e. \(\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_t = \tilde{A}_1 \tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{t-1} + \tilde{\mathbf{e}}_t\). So it suffices to study the \(\mathsf{VAR}(1)\).

Stacking lags turns any \(\mathsf{VAR}(p)\) into a \(\mathsf{VAR}(1)\) — so we focus on the latter.

2 Structural VAR models

Economists care not just about econometric equations but about primitive structural shocks (e.g. monetary policy, technology) and their dynamic causal effects. Consider a three-variable macro system — output \(X_t\), inflation \(\pi_t\), interest rate \(i_t\) — with \(\mathbf{y}_t = (X_t, \pi_t, i_t)'\) and \(\mathbf{y}_t = A_1 \mathbf{y}_{t-1} + \mathbf{e}_t\).

Econometric errors versus structural shocks

The econometric error \(e_{i,t}\) is simply the unexplained portion of variable \(i\) at time \(t\). These are not the same as the structural shocks \(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t\) — the true, primitive drivers of the economy (a monetary-policy shock, a technology shock, etc.), whose effects on the system are unobserved. The identification problem is to recover both the structural shocks and their dynamic effects (impulse responses) from econometric data alone.

Reduced-form residuals are a statistical by-product; structural shocks are the economic objects of interest.

What is the identification problem?

For a causal stationary VAR, \(A(L)\mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{e}_t\) with \(\mathbf{e}_t \sim \mathsf{WN}(\mathbf{0}, \Sigma_e)\), we can invert to the reduced-form moving-average representation \[ \mathbf{y}_t = B(L)\mathbf{e}_t, \qquad B(L) = A^{-1}(L). \] But what we want is the structural representation \(\mathbf{y}_t = C(L)\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t\), where \(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t\) are the structural shocks and \(C(L)\) contains the impulse response functions (IRFs).

The difficulty is rotational indeterminacy: for any orthogonal matrix \(P\) (with \(P'P = I_N\)), \[ \mathbf{y}_t = B(L)P'P\,\mathbf{e}_t = \Gamma(L)\,\mathbf{u}_t, \] leaving the covariance structure of \(\mathbf{y}_t\) unchanged. Requiring \(\mathsf{Var}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t) = I_N\) imposes the \(N(N+1)/2\) symmetric constraints in \(\Sigma_e\), leaving \(N^2 - N(N+1)/2 = N(N-1)/2\) degrees of freedom that must be pinned down by economic restrictions.

The data fix the covariance structure but not the rotation — economics must supply the remaining \(N(N-1)/2\) restrictions.

Setting \(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t = P\mathbf{e}_t\) (so \(\mathsf{Var}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t) = I_N\)) and \(C(L) = B(L)P^{-1}\), we expand \[ \mathbf{y}_t = C_0\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t + C_1\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{t-1} + C_2\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{t-2} + \cdots, \qquad C_j = B_j P^{-1}. \] Two matrices are of special interest: \(C_0 = C(0)\), the short-run (instantaneous) responses, and \(C(1) = \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} C_j\), the long-run (cumulative) responses.

Short-run restrictions — Sims (1980)

We impose restrictions on \(C_0\). We have exactly \(N(N-1)/2\) remaining degrees of freedom — precisely the number of below-diagonal entries of an \(N \times N\) matrix. A Cholesky decomposition delivers this structure:

  1. Factor \(\Sigma_e = LL'\), with \(L\) lower triangular (positive diagonal).
  2. Set \(P = L^{-1}\).
  3. Then \(P'P = (LL')^{-1} = \Sigma_e^{-1}\) (orthogonalisation) and \(C_0 = B_0 P^{-1} = L\) is lower triangular.

For the ordering \((X_t, \pi_t, i_t)\), \[ \begin{pmatrix} X_t \\ \pi_t \\ i_t \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} c_{11}^{(0)} & 0 & 0 \\ c_{21}^{(0)} & c_{22}^{(0)} & 0 \\ c_{31}^{(0)} & c_{32}^{(0)} & c_{33}^{(0)} \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} \epsilon_{1,t} \\ \epsilon_{2,t} \\ \epsilon_{3,t} \end{pmatrix} + \cdots \] The ordering encodes which shocks may have instantaneous effects: a variable ordered first cannot be contemporaneously affected by other shocks. (If \(\epsilon_{3,t}\) is a monetary-policy shock, the third column of \(C(L)\) holds its IRFs.)

Cholesky → lower-triangular \(C_0\); the variable ordering is the identifying assumption.

Long-run restrictions — Blanchard & Quah (1989)

Instead of restricting \(C_0\), we restrict \(C(1)\) — the long-run cumulative responses. If a variable enters in differences (e.g. \(\Delta \ln X_t\)), \(C(1)\) captures the cumulative effect on the level; a demand shock may have only transitory effects on output, while a technology shock has a permanent one.

For a bivariate system, choose \(P\) so that (i) \(P'P = \Sigma_e^{-1}\) and (ii) \(C(1)\) is lower triangular. The procedure: Cholesky-factor \(B(1)\Sigma_e B(1)' = QQ'\) (with \(Q\) lower triangular), set \(P = Q^{-1}B(1)\), and obtain \(C(L) = B(L)P^{-1}\). One verifies \(P'P = \Sigma_e^{-1}\) and \(C(1) = B(1)P^{-1} = Q\), lower triangular — encoding long-run neutrality (e.g. \(\sum_{j} c_{12}^{(j)} = 0\)).

Same orthogonalisation, but the triangular restriction is imposed on the long-run matrix \(C(1)\), not on \(C_0\).

What is the forecast-error variance decomposition (FEVD)?

With an identified VAR, \(\mathbf{x}_t = C(L)\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t\) and \(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t \sim \mathsf{WN}(\mathbf{0}, I_N)\), the \(s\)-period-ahead forecast error is \[ \mathbf{x}_{t+s} - \mathsf{E}(\mathbf{x}_{t+s}\mid\mathcal{F}_t) = \sum_{j=0}^{s-1} C_j \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{t+s-j}. \] The proportion of the \(s\)-period-ahead forecast-error variance of variable \(i\) attributable to shock \(k\) is \[ \pi_{ik}^{(s)} = \frac{\sum_{j=0}^{s-1} \big(c_{ik}^{(j)}\big)^2}{\sum_{j=0}^{s-1}\sum_{k=1}^{N} \big(c_{ik}^{(j)}\big)^2}, \qquad \text{with } \sum_{k=1}^{N} \pi_{ik}^{(s)} = 1. \] The FEVD tells us how important each structural shock is for explaining the variation in each variable over a given horizon.

The FEVD apportions each variable’s forecast uncertainty across the structural shocks.

NoteGranger causation (not examinable)

A pragmatic definition. Despite the name, Granger causality is not a deep notion of true causation. Granger’s two components are: (1) the cause occurs before the effect; and (2) the cause contains unique information about the effect not present in any other variable. Formally, \(x_{1,t}\) Granger causes \(x_{2,t}\) if, for at least one \(s > 0\), the MSE of a forecast of \(x_{2,t+s}\) based on \((x_{2,t}, x_{2,t-1}, \ldots)\) alone is strictly greater than the MSE of a forecast also using \((x_{1,t}, x_{1,t-1}, \ldots)\). (A weather forecast may Granger-cause rainfall — the forecast precedes the rain and adds information — but it obviously does not cause the rain.)

In the VAR. For a bivariate \(\mathsf{VAR}(p)\) with lag-\(j\) matrices \(A_j = (a^{(j)}_{kl})\): if \(a_{12}^{(j)} = 0\) for all \(j\) (i.e. \(A(z)\) is lower triangular), then \(x_{2,t}\) fails to Granger cause \(x_{1,t}\). One tests \(H_0: a_{21}^{(j)} = 0\) (for all \(j\)) via an \(F\)-test; rejection implies \(x_{1,t}\) Granger causes \(x_{2,t}\).

Conceptual problems. (i) Bidirectional causality — if both \(a_{12}^{(j)} \neq 0\) and \(a_{21}^{(j)} \neq 0\), each Granger-causes the other (uninformative). (ii) Confounding — an unobserved third variable affecting both with different delays can create spurious Granger causality. (iii) Non-transitivity — \(x_3 \to x_2\) and \(x_2 \to x_1\) need not imply \(x_3 \to x_1\) at one step, though an indirect effect \(a_{12}a_{23}\) appears at longer horizons.

Granger causal priority (GCP). To address these, \(x_{1,t}\) is Granger causal prior to \(x_{3,t}\) if the variables can be partitioned into blocks \(\mathcal{B}_1 \ni x_{1,t}\) and \(\mathcal{B}_3 \ni x_{3,t}\) such that \(\mathcal{B}_3\) fails to (block-) Granger cause \(\mathcal{B}_1\).

NoteAppendix — counting the restrictions

For an \(N \times N\) rotation \(P\), orthogonalisation (\(\mathsf{Var}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t) = I_N\), with \(\Sigma_e\) symmetric) imposes \(N(N+1)/2\) restrictions. Identification then requires a further \(N(N-1)/2\) restrictions (supplied by Cholesky ordering, long-run neutrality, etc.), for a total of \[ \frac{N(N+1)}{2} + \frac{N(N-1)}{2} = N^2 \] restrictions — exactly the number of free parameters in \(P\). (For \(N = 2\): \(3 + 1 = 4\).)

3 Review questions

  1. Write a bivariate \(\mathsf{VAR}(1)\) in scalar, vector, and matrix-lag-polynomial form. State the condition under which it is weakly stationary and causal.
  2. Explain the identification problem in a structural VAR. Why are exactly \(N(N-1)/2\) additional (economic) restrictions required?
  3. Contrast short-run (Sims, 1980) and long-run (Blanchard & Quah, 1989) identification. On which matrix — \(C_0\) or \(C(1)\) — does each impose triangularity?
  4. Define the forecast-error variance decomposition \(\pi_{ik}^{(s)}\) and explain what it measures.
